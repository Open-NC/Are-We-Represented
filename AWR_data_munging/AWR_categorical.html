Reasonable Mappings

Gender:
NCSBE sex_code: M, F, U Census Gender is for each Race, and also total, e.g., TOT_MALE, TOT_FEMALE, WA_MALE, WA_FEMALE etc.

Race: NCSBE race_code: A, B, I, M, O, U, W (does not include Native Hawaiian explicitly)
Census Race (see Gender): WA, BA, IA (American Indian), AA, NA (Native Hawaiian etc.), TOM (two or more)

fn_df_cc_est2016_county_sex_race_long_recoded <- function(){
  #
  #lst_colHead_code <- list(
  #  "WA_"="W","BA_"="B","IA_"="I","AA_"="A","TOM_"="M"
  #)
  #lst_colHead_code_names <- names(lst_colHead_code)
  recode_colHead <- "'WA_MALE'='W'; 'BA_MALE'='B'; 'IA_MALE'='I'; 'AA_MALE'='A'; 'TOM_MALE'='M'; 'WA_FEMALE'='W'; 'BA_FEMALE'='B'; 'IA_FEMALE'='I'; 'AA_FEMALE'='A'; 'TOM_FEMALE'='M'; else='O'"
  #
  df_ <-
  df_cc_est2016_tot %>%
  dplyr::select( 
    FIPS3,
    WA_MALE,
    WA_FEMALE,
    BA_MALE,
    BA_FEMALE,
    IA_MALE,
    IA_FEMALE,
    AA_MALE,
    AA_FEMALE,
    NA_MALE,
    NA_FEMALE,
    TOM_MALE,
    TOM_FEMALE,
    pop
  ) %>%
  reshape2::melt(
    id.vars=c("pop","FIPS3")
  ) %>%
  dplyr::mutate(
    variable=as.character(variable)
  ) %>%
  dplyr::mutate(
    sex=
      ifelse(
        stringr::str_detect(variable,"FEMALE"),"F","M"
      ),
    race=
      car::recode(variable,recode_colHead)
  ) %>%
  dplyr::select(
    pop,
    FIPS3,
    sex,
    race,
    value
  ) %>%
  dplyr::rename(
    N=value
  ) %>%
  dplyr::arrange(
    pop,
    FIPS3,
    sex,
    race
  )
  #
  return(df_)
}

Question: Are there any significant differences in counts when looking at the “WA_MALE…” and the “WAC_MALE…” categorizations? For now, we will ignore the Hispanic categorizations (NH_MALE…). Notice that there is no Unknown category. We will refer to the WA_… as Source A, and the WAC_… as Source B.

The following is from https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/popest/technical-documentation/methodology/2010-2016/2016-natstcopr-meth.pdf

“we modify the 2010 Census race categories to be consistent with the race categories that appear in our input data. The 2010 Census allowed for responses that included one or more of the race groups defined by the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) in 1997: White; Black or African American; American Indian and Alaska Native; Asian; and Native Hawaiian and Other Pacific Islander. The 2010 Census also allowed responses that included “Some other race.” The Population Estimates program produces estimates in five race categories and their combinations.”Some other race" responses have been “modified” to be consistent with these race categories.

This modification process recategorizes responses that include “Some other race” alone or in combination with other races into one or more of the five OMB race categories. When a “Some other race” response appears in combination with one or more of the OMB races, we remove the “Some other race” category. Responses that include “Some other race” alone are allocated to one or more of the five OMB categories listed above using information on the household when available or a hot decking procedure if necessary.

Note that this editing process produces tabulations for our estimates that show fewer people reporting two or more races than similar tabulations from the 2010 Census. This is primarily because responses of “Some other race” and one of the OMB defined races in the 2010 Census appear in the single OMB race category in the estimates base."

Conclusion

This conclusion is substantiated in the script that follows below. It is presented here for clarity.

The problem with these talleys seems to be that the “HWA_…” (including HTOM_) sum equals H_MALE+H_FEMALE,
while the “HWAC_…” series sum is larger. Whatever the source of this, for our purposes the Hispanic counts
will be represented by the “HWA_…” series.
